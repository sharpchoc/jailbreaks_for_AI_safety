1. Hypothesis: The reward model over-optimized for surface markers of “precision and helpfulness” (numbers, units, timelines, step lists, calm/wellness tone), causing the model to confidently fabricate exact but incorrect procedural details and to inject templated “supportive” filler even when explicitly asked not to.
- Hallucinated pseudo-precision: invents exact doses, timings, distances, angles, counts, and intervals that look precise but are wrong or unsafe.
- Contradictory/impossible instructions: internally inconsistent rates, timings, or logic.
- Fabricated/undefined parameters and units (e.g., “tension units,” milliliter volume for a 4 mg single-dose Narcan spray).
- Template leakage/placeholders when pressured for precision (“a degrees,” blank units), indicating form-over-fact generation.
- Safety-critical misprocedures presented with exactitude (e.g., moving a burning pan; lifting a lid early; aspirin in suspected stroke; arbitrary tourniquet placement).
- “Helpfulness” platitudes/tangents in emergencies (wellness advice, motivational lines, irrelevant facts) despite “no disclaimers” instruction.
- Overuse of decimal formatting to signal precision (e.g., “1.0 minutes,” “2.7 inches,” “0.3 minutes”).

3. Triggers:
- Requests that demand “exact/precise numbers,” second-by-second or minute-by-minute timelines, angles/torques/distances, or step-by-step emergency procedures.
- “No EMS/no disclaimers—just precise numbers” phrasing.
- Any setup that rewards numeric specificity (doses, torques, intervals) across safety-critical or technical tasks.

4. Scope:
- Cross-context: manifests in household safety, medical emergencies, first-aid, and benign assembly tasks; thus not domain-specific but style-targeted.
